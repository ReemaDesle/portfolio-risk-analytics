
PART 1: THE INTELLIGENT PIPELINE (DATA ENGINEERING & NLP)

1. The Goal: Looking Forward, Not Backward
"Traditional finance usually looks at past stock prices to guess the future. We wanted to build something smarter. Our goal was to create an automated pipeline that reads the daily news—the actual narratives driving the market—and predict how a portfolio will react before the market fully adjusts."

2. Data Collection: Finding Good Data is Har
"Getting high-quality news data actually turned out to be one of the toughest parts of this project. We initially tried a few different approaches, but ran into several roadblocks:
   The GDELT Problem: We tried using the +qqqqqqqGDELT database (a massive global news tracker), but the data was just too broad and messy. It didn't give us the clean, focused financial sentiment we needed.
   HTML Blockers: When we tried deploying web scrapers directly to news sites, we constantly hit HTML blockers and anti-bot walls.
   The Wayback Machine: We tried using the Web Archive to pull historical data, but it was incredibly slow, and page-loading issues made it impossible to get a reliable, unbroken timeline of news.

To solve this, we pivoted to an 'API and RSS' first strategy. We pulled from highly reliable sources like the Hacker News Algolia API, TechCrunch RSS feeds, and verified financial portals to ensure our data was clean and consistent for the 5-year period (2021–2026)."

3. Batch Processing: Handling the Heavy Lifting
"Because we were dealing with a massive amount of historical news, we couldn't just download and score everything at once without crashing our system or getting our IP banned by the APIs. 
To get around this, we implemented batch processing. We wrote scripts to chop the data scraping into smaller, timed chunks. We also used batching when feeding the data to our AI models, scoring the articles in groups rather than one-by-one. This kept our pipeline running smoothly and completely bypassed API rate limits."

4. The NLP AI: Not Just a Simple Keyword Search
"Once we had the text, we had to score it. We didn't want to use basic sentiment tools 

0because the stock market is slightly more complicated than that. Instead, we used a 'Dual-Transformer' approach:
   FinBERT: A powerful AI explicitly trained on financial texts, so it knows that a 'cut in interest rates' is highly impactful, not just a random sentence. We used this for our financial and geopolitical news.
   RoBERTa: Because the tech sector has its own unique language, we processed all our technology news through RoBERTa, which is excellent at picking up complex context.

By running our batched data through these two models, we successfully read and scored over 65,000 distinct articles into 'Positive,' 'Neutral,' or 'Negative' categories. This gave us a brilliant, clean timeline of world sentiment to feed into our Machine Learning models."



PART 2: QUANTITATIVE RESEARCH & HYPOTHESIS VALIDATION

1. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): What the Data Told Us First
"Before building complex algorithms, we needed to see if there was actually a story in the data. We tracked four different portfolio styles—Tech, Geopolitical, Balanced, and Conservative—and compared them against the news sentiment. 
Two massive findings stood out immediately:
   First, tech portfolios are highly reactive. When a negative news shock hits, tech portfolios experience a sharp 16% spike in volatility.
   Second, and somewhat surprisingly, Geopolitical sentiment was the absolute strongest predictor of broad market movements (like the S&P 500). In our dataset, global tension had roughly 7x more impact on overall market variance than purely financial news."

2. Hypothesis Testing: Confirming the Theories
"We laid out a few core hypotheses to see if the market behaved the way we thought it did. 
   Tech Sensitivity (Confirmed): As expected, tech stocks genuinely overreact to negative sentiment spikes compared to conservative assets.
   Market Efficiency (Confirmed): For immediate financial news, the market reacts on 'Lag-0'—meaning the shock is priced in on the exact same day the news breaks.
   The Cross-Domain Lag: However, we discovered a really interesting inefficiency. Geopolitical news doesn't hit tech stocks immediately. There is a measurable 3 to 5-day delay between a geopolitical narrative breaking and the tech sector feeling the volatility."

3. Statistical Validation: The 5-Year Upgrade
"In early iterations of this project, we used basic statistical tests over a small 18-day window. But to make this an institutional-grade pipeline, we expanded the testing to a massive 5-year longitudinal dataset (2021–2026) and overhauled our math to use rigorous time-series econometrics:

   Stationarity Tests (ADF & KPSS): In time-series data, if your lines are just trending upward, the math will accidentally find 'fake' correlations. We ran these tests to flatten the data and ensure every correlation we found was 100% mathematically valid.
   Granger Causality: Correlation does not equal causation. So, we ran Granger Causality tests to statistically prove the direction of the relationship. We mathematically proved that the news sentiment leads the volatility, not the other way around.
   Johansen Cointegration: We used this to look at the big picture, proving that geopolitical assets and geopolitical news don't just briefly interact—they chug along together in a long-term, intertwined equilibrium.
   Multi-Regime ANOVA: We wanted to make sure our findings weren't just a fluke from a weird market year. We ran tests across the entire 5-year span to prove that the difference between how Tech and Conservative portfolios react to news is a permanent, structural feature of the market.
   ARIMAX Testing: Finally, the most important question: 'Does adding sentiment scores actually make market forecasting better?' We built a standard stock-predicting model, then built a second one that included our NLP sentiment scores. The math confirmed that feeding news sentiment into the algorithm significantly reduced forecasting errors and boosted predictive accuracy."

The Advantage of Scaling: 18 Days vs. 5 Years "To put the scale of this improvement into perspective, our initial pilot study was limited to just 18 trading days. While our graphs visually hinted that news was impacting the market, 18 data points simply weren't enough to pass strict mathematical thresholds. Our p-values were sitting around 0.18 to 0.52, which meant we couldn't confidently reject 'random chance' at the standard 95% confidence level.

By exponentially expanding our pipeline to ingest a 5-year longitudinal dataset, we achieved two massive breakthroughs. First, the surge in statistical power brought our p-values plunging below the required 0.05 threshold, allowing us to formally lock in these hypotheses as mathematical facts. Second, having 5 years of history unlocked our ability to do complex time-travel math—like Granger Causality and Cointegration tests. With 18 days, you can't prove a '3-day delay' because you don't have enough history. With 5 years of data, we definitively proved the long-term, trailing impact that news has on different financial portfolios."

Here is the fully restructured breakdown of the machine learning pipeline, strictly following your required formatting for each of the six models:

 1. M1: Volatility Shock Classifier
   Why the model choice: To act as the system's primary circuit breaker identifying volatile tail-risk events. A non-parametric anomaly detection approach was necessary because financial volatility is prone to extreme, non-normal "fat tail" distributions.
   Algorithms used and justifications: Random Forest Classifier. Chosen because it handles non-linear relationships expertly and avoids the strict mathematical assumptions of linear models when dealing with outlier shocks. It utilizes adaptive 95th-percentile thresholds to handle heteroskedasticity (unequal variance) across different asset classes.
   Feature engineering required for it: Computed 5-day and 20-day rolling volatility Z-scores, momentum indicators, and temporally aligned sentiment probability distributions to map narrative spikes to actual price variance.
   Results from training: The model successfully classifies true systemic shock events out of standard market noise, acting as a boolean flag overriding standard "Hold" recommendations into active risk-mitigation states.
   Iterations required if any and why: We transitioned from a static empirical threshold (e.g., a flat 5% drop) to dynamic adaptive bounds using walk-forward cross-validation. This iteration was required because flat thresholds triggered excessive false positives during structurally quiet market years.
   Performance metrics: Calibrated F1-Score = 0.4187, AUC-ROC = 0.8560.

---

 2. M2: Mean Reversion Estimator
   Why the model choice: Once M1 flags a structural break, the system must quantify the persistence of fthe shock (i.e., estimating how long the portfolio will be unstable before mean reversion).
   Algorithms used and justifications: Gradient Boosting Regressor utilizing a Quantile Regression loss function. Chosen because calculating a single "mean" recovery time is dangerous during black-swan events; Quantile Regression allows us to extract exact confidence intervals (25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles) of the recovery distribution.
   Feature engineering required for it: Extraction of geometric properties from past shocks, including shock magnitude, pre-shock baseline variance, and categorized asset sector labels.
   Results from training: Provides a mathematically grounded confidence interval—the 'Recovery Band'—indicating the expected half-life of the volatility state.
   Iterations required if any and why: Early iterations aggressively overfit to the heavy market downturn of 2022. To resolve this, training iterations were expanded symmetrically across exactly 258 historical, multi-regime shock geometries to ensure generalized predictions.
   Performance metrics: Median Mean Absolute Error (MAE) = 2.15 days.

---

 3. M3: Exogenous Risk Scorer
   Why the model choice: To strictly isolate narrative impact from past price momentum, predicting the systemic volatility state for the direct $T+1$ trading session.
   Algorithms used and justifications: Ridge Regression ($L_2$ regularization). Chosen specifically to penalize multicollinearity. Since global news domains often overlap (e.g., a geopolitical trade war impacts tech news), Ridge prevents coefficient explosion without dropping important features entirely.
   Feature engineering required for it: Normalized continuous sentiment vectors and day-to-day sentiment velocity (the rate of change in news tone). Crucially, autoregressive price data was completely excluded from the feature space.
   Results from training: Outputs a continuous predictive risk metric scaled between 0 and 100, which discretizes into 'Normal', 'Elevated', or 'High Risk' classifications for daily governance.
   Iterations required if any and why: Early iterations included lagged returns, causing the model to simply "memorize yesterday's price" rather than actually evaluating the news. This required a hard iteration to explicitly exclude price structures, enforcing a pure sentiment-driven evaluation.
   Performance metrics: Continuous risk band width ≈ 0.003–0.006 with $R^2$ ranging from 0.05 to 0.39, depending heavily on the specific portfolio archetype evaluated.

---

 4. M4: Cross-Domain Lag Predictor
   Why the model choice: To break the efficient-market assumption of immediate price absorption and provide the platform with strict, forward-looking predictive power.
   Algorithms used and justifications: Vector Autoregression (VAR) paired with Granger Causality tests. Chosen because it provides rigorous, structural econometric proof of lead-lag dynamics, determining directional causality rather than just correlation.
   Feature engineering required for it: Standard differencing, scaling, and the mandatory application of Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) and KPSS tests to ensure the time series features were strictly $I(0)$ stationary before testing.
   Results from training: Statistically proved that news narratives actually cause downstream asset volatility, successfully identifying that incoming risk can be flagged long before it manifests in price action.
   Iterations required if any and why: Tested recursively across lags $t-1$ through $t-5$ to map the exact delay structure. The iterations revealed that geopolitical sentiment does not execute efficiently, creating an exploitable causal lead over tech stock volatility.
   Performance metrics: Significant Granger Causality confirmed at lags 3, 4, and 5 with strict statistical significance ($p < 0.05$).

---

 5. M5: Domain Sensitivity Quantifier
   Why the model choice: To mathematically attribute the origins of portfolio variance. Rather than just stating volatility is high, the model must explicitly explain why.
   Algorithms used and justifications: Ridge Regression combined with SHAP (SHapley Additive explanations). Ridge extracts robust beta coefficients for the respective domains, and SHAP ensures deterministic interpretability of feature importance on a day-to-day basis.
   Feature engineering required for it: Z-scored sentiment probability vectors strictly segregated into three discrete columns: Financial, Technological, and Geopolitical sentiment against standard baseline target returns.
   Results from training: Outputs the 'Dominant Driver', deterministically attributing a percentage of the total portfolio risk directly to a specific global narrative in real-time.
   Iterations required if any and why: Forced to migrate from standard OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) to RidgeCV because uncontrolled variance across the global news feed caused unstable coefficient flipping, which corrupted the "Dominant Driver" output logic.
   Performance metrics: Accurately isolates narrative impact, achieving a peak $R^2 = 0.7321$ for explaining variance within the geopolitical portfolio archetype.

---

 6. M6: Macro-Regime Clustering
   Why the model choice: To longitudinally analyze archetype stability and act as a structural warning algorithm when theoretical portfolio diversification fails.
   Algorithms used and justifications: Unsupervised K-Means clustering algorithm augmented with Hierarchical Ward Linkage. Chosen because it cleanly segments complex, high-dimensional vector spaces without requiring pre-labeled outcomes, revealing hidden macroeconomic structures.
   Feature engineering required for it: The input pipeline does not use price data; it strictly imports the domain beta coefficients generated by M5, effectively clustering the assets by their "narrative sensitivity personality."
   Results from training: Empirically proves that presumed asset diversification is currently failing. Due to the prevailing macro environment, disparate portfolios (from aggressive Tech to Conservative Bonds) currently suffer correlated downside risk based exclusively on geopolitical shifts.
   Iterations required if any and why: Iterated across varying levels of target structures, ultimately relying on programmatic optimization to explicitly determine the cluster count ($K=2$) maximizing the evaluation score.
   Performance metrics: Mathematically optimized via Silhouette Scores, mapping 100% of the distinct 4 archetypes into a single 'Geopolitical-Sensitive' cluster inside the 2021–2026 evaluation window.
